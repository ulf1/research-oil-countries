# Global oil map 2025/2026: sanctions, shale plateaus, and a pre-salt/Guyana surge

**Global crude output held near 102–103 mb/d through 2025 even as the geopolitical architecture of the market fractured.** The United States stayed atop the league table at ~13.6 mb/d but moved into its first forecast decline in five years; Saudi Arabia and Russia re-entered managed growth as OPEC+ unwound 2.2 mb/d of voluntary cuts between April and September 2025; Guyana crossed 900 kb/d for the first time; and sanctioned barrels from Russia, Iran and Venezuela — roughly 13–14 mb/d combined — were routed through a shadow fleet that industry trackers now size at anywhere from 600 to over 3,000 vessels. The October 22, 2025 OFAC designation of **Rosneft and Lukoil**, the EU's **19th sanctions package** a day later (cutting the Russian oil price cap to **$47.60/bbl**), and Washington's December naval "quarantine" of Venezuelan exports were the defining supply-side events of the year. Underneath those headlines sits a quieter structural story: Permian efficiency is flatlining, Canada's TMX has re-oriented North American flows toward Asia, Qatar, the UAE and Saudi Arabia are sprinting toward a combined 60+ mtpa of new LNG and 5 mb/d-plus of crude capacity by 2027, and new Atlantic-basin barrels from Guyana, Suriname, Brazil and Namibia are quietly reshaping the non-OPEC supply stack.

The report below organises the world's producers into the five clusters requested, with company-level ownership, 2025 production, fiscal dependence on oil, rig activity where disclosed, and the most material upstream and infrastructure projects of 2025/2026.

**Shadow fleet and discounted routes.** Estimates of the Russia-linked shadow/dark fleet diverge by an order of magnitude — **CSIS counts 155–591 dedicated tankers**, while **S&P Global says 3,154 ships** (≈48% of the global ≥27,000-dwt tanker fleet) have lifted sanctioned Russian cargo since December 2022, and the Kyiv Independent puts current active shadow tonnage at ~3,240 vessels moving 337 million barrels a month. The EU had sanctioned **600 vessels** by the end of 2025 (557 in the 19th package plus 41 more in Council Reg. 2025/2618 on Dec 18, and 9 enablers on Dec 15); the UK had sanctioned ~500 and the US ~216. STS activity has migrated from the **Lakonikos Gulf** — where 85% of traffic dispersed after Greek naval exercises in May 2024 — to the **Malta lightering area (44%)**, **Augusta, Sicily (9%)**, **Lome, Togo (7%)**, Ceuta and Kalamata, with Murmansk and Kozmino as Russia-side hubs. Flagging is concentrated in Panama, Liberia, Marshall Islands, Cook Islands, Gabon, Djibouti (widely fraudulent) and Cameroon.

Iran's "Ghost Armada" overlaps partially with Russia's fleet and runs through the **Malaysia/South China Sea EOPL zone**, where tens of millions of barrels sit in floating storage. UANI tracked 51 vessels moving Iranian oil in November 2025, 70% of them VLCC/ULCC class, and 38 of those had US sanctions on them yet continued trading — the clearest illustration of the enforcement gap. Kharg Island handled **all** of Iran's crude exports (1.83 mb/d to China in November, per FDD); UAE transshipped 5.9%, Malaysia 1.5%. Total 2025 Iranian export revenue came in around **$45.7 bn** (UANI vessel-tracking) versus a customs-based $7.7 bn from TradeInt — the UANI number is the credible one. Venezuelan Merey, already the worst-placed heavy grade in the sanctioned basket, blew out to **–$21/bbl** against Brent in December after the US Navy seized the tanker *Skipper* (1.85 million barrels) on Dec 10, 2025 and idled 19 laden tankers carrying 11 million barrels in Venezuelan waters.

**2025/2026 sanctions architecture.** The coalition fragmented: the US under Trump retained the original **$60 G7 price cap** while the EU and UK cut theirs to **$47.60 (effective Sept 3, 2025)** with a 15%-below-market dynamic mechanism. Biden's January 10, 2025 SDN action on **Gazprom Neft, Surgutneftegas, Sovcomflot and ~180 tankers** was followed by Trump's first Russia move — the **October 22, 2025 blocking of Rosneft and Lukoil** plus 30 subsidiaries, accompanied by general licences GL 124A (CPC/Tengiz carve-out), GL 126 (Nov 21 wind-down), GL 128 (Lukoil retail abroad) and GL 131 (Lukoil divestment to Dec 13). The **EU 18th package** (July 18, 2025) sanctioned India's Nayara Energy (49% Rosneft) and 22 Russian plus 2 Chinese banks; the **19th package** (Oct 23, 2025) added 12 Chinese companies including Liaoyang Petrochemical and Shandong Yulong. A ban on refined products derived from Russian crude in third countries took effect **Jan 21, 2026**, and the **20th package** is expected around Feb 23, 2026, targeting third-country logistics hubs in Georgia and Indonesia. On Venezuela, Trump flipped Chevron's Biden-era GL 41 via **GL 41A (Feb 26, 2025, wind-down to April 3)** but then issued a new in-kind payment licence in July 2025 that let Chevron ramp from 128 kb/d in October to 150 kb/d in November and up to 240 kb/d — now the country's single largest producer outside PDVSA.

## Cluster B — Arab Gulf states (GCC)

On **April 3, 2025**, eight OPEC+ members launched the phased unwinding of 2.2 mb/d of voluntary cuts originally spread over 18 months. They compressed the schedule aggressively — **May, June and July each added 411 kb/d, August 548 kb/d** — and by September had effectively restored the 2.2 mb/d. The group then **paused further increases for Q1 2026** in November 2025 after Brent slipped to $60.

**Spare capacity** is overwhelmingly Saudi — roughly 2.5 mb/d sits below the Kingdom's 12 mb/d MSC — with the UAE holding ~1.5 mb/d, Kuwait ~0.6 mb/d, and almost nothing elsewhere. The quota-capacity gap is driving an assertive push by **the UAE and Kuwait** for higher OPEC+ baselines in the 2027 reference-level reset; a new mechanism to assess members' maximum capacity was formally approved in 2025.

**Vision 2030 / diversification.** Saudi Arabia's **2026 budget is SAR 1.313 trillion ($350 bn)** with a 3.3% deficit, and PIF assets have reached $1.15 trn (target $2.67 trn by 2030). **NEOM/The Line was formally suspended in September 2025** after only 2.4 km of foundation was built; the original $1.6 trillion cost had ballooned internally to $8.8 trn, and the 2030 population target was cut from 1.5 m to under 300 k. PIF is now rerouting capital toward AI infrastructure (the $5 bn DataVolt deal of February 2026). The UAE's **ADNOC approved a new $150 bn (AED 551 bn) capex plan for 2026–2030** in November 2025, on top of the existing $150 bn 2023–27 programme; $23 bn of that goes to decarbonisation. Kuwait is executing a **$410 bn 2040 strategy** with $100 bn over the next five years, anchored by the **615 kb/d Al-Zour refinery** (full tilt in 2024) and a target of **4 mb/d by 2035**. Qatar's **North Field programme** takes LNG capacity from 77 mtpa today to **126 mtpa by 2027** (NFE 32 mtpa + NFS 16 mtpa) and **142 mtpa by end-2030** with the 16-mtpa NFW addition, for which Chiyoda won the FEED contract in January 2026. Oman's 2026 budget assumes $60/bbl and targets cutting oil GDP dependence from 30% to under 10% by 2040 via the **$15 bn Duqm SEZ**, the **OQ8 refinery (Duqm, now 255 kb/d, mulling 280)** and **Marsa LNG** at Sohar (groundbreaking May 2025). Bahrain's **$7 bn Bapco Modernization Programme** lifts Sitra refinery from 267 to 380 kb/d, completing commissioning through 2025; it is the GCC's structurally weakest producer, with the IMF projecting a **9.9% of GDP fiscal deficit in 2026**.

**Upstream and export infrastructure.** Aramco's **Jafurah unconventional gas** ($100 bn NPV, 229 Tcf) came onstream at **450 mmscf/d in late 2025** and targets 2 bscfd of sales gas by 2030; Phase 2 contracts worth $12.4 bn were awarded in 2025, and the $11 bn lease-leaseback with GIP/BlackRock closed in August. The **Marjan (+300 kb/d) and Berri (+250 kb/d) increments** and the **Tanajib Gas Plant (+2.6 bscfd)** all onstreamed in 2025; the **Zuluf 600 kb/d central facility** is due in 2026. ADNOC's **Hail & Ghasha** ($17 bn EPC, 1.8 bscfd + 150 kb/d) is now ADNOC Ghasha, secured an **$11 bn non-recourse financing** (oversubscribed 1.5×) on Dec 18, 2025, and will be the world's first net-zero upstream project with 1.5 mtpa of CO₂ capture. **Ruwais LNG** (two 4.8 mtpa trains) has Shell, BP, Total and Mitsui each at 10% and is the first LNG plant region-wide powered by nuclear and renewables. For Hormuz risk, Saudi's **East-West Pipeline (Petroline)** was raised to a claimed **7 mb/d in March 2025**, and the UAE's **ADCOP (Habshan-Fujairah, 1.5 mb/d)** will be doubled by a second 1.5 mb/d, 500-km line targeted for 2026–27. Kuwait, Qatar and Bahrain have no material bypass capacity.

**Shale efficiency.** The Permian is producing **6.6 mb/d from 241 rigs** in early 2026 — rig count is down 29% since December 2022, yet output is up 18% (~1 mb/d). But the efficiency frontier is visibly bending: Dallas Fed's 2024–25 survey puts **Permian Midland breakeven at $62/bbl** (Delaware $64), and Rystad's all-in corporate cash-flow breakeven (incl. dividends, debt service and an 18% hurdle rate) sits at **$62.50/bbl WTI**. At the majors, RBC pegs Exxon's dividend-plus-buyback breakeven at **$88** and Chevron's at **$95**. Kpler warns that sustained $50 WTI would cut the US rig count to 360 and take 700 kb/d off 2026 output. The consolidation wave is essentially complete: **Exxon–Pioneer ($64.5 bn, May 2024)**, **Chevron–Hess (~$55 bn, mid-2025)**, **Diamondback–Endeavor ($26 bn, Sep 2024)**, **ConocoPhillips–Marathon ($22.5 bn, Nov 2024)** and **Oxy–CrownRock (~$12 bn)** together moved more than $250 bn of acreage.

**Pipeline infrastructure.** Permian takeaway is at **~91% utilisation**: Wink-to-Webster (1.5 mb/d, Exxon/Plains/MPLX), Gray Oak (900 kb/d + 120 kb/d expansion end-2025, Enbridge/P66), Cactus II (~670 kb/d), Midland-to-Echo (~1.5 mb/d), EPIC (600 kb/d), plus **Matterhorn gas (2.5 Bcf/d, 2024 in-service)**. Of the four planned VLCC-capable **Gulf deepwater export terminals** — SPOT (Enterprise, 2 mb/d), Bluewater (P66/Trafigura), Blue Marlin (Energy Transfer) and Texas GulfLink — **SPOT is now "in limbo"** on insufficient customer interest (OGJ, Feb 2025); none will be operational before 2027–28. In Canada, **TMX is the single most consequential asset**: its 890 kb/d of combined capacity ran at 82–88% utilisation in 2024–25 (peaking at 793 kb/d in March 2025), and the **Enbridge Mainline Optimization Phase 1 FID in November 2025** will add 150 kb/d on the Mainline plus 100 kb/d on Flanagan South for $1.4 bn; Phase 2 adds 250 kb/d by the end of the decade. The US share of Canadian crude exports collapsed from 93.8% pre-TMX to **51.9%** in the first year after start-up, with **China now the largest single TMX destination** (353,674 b/d in March 2025). Mexico's **Dos Bocas/Olmeca** refinery processed only 118 kb/d January–October 2025 against 340 kb/d nameplate; total Pemex refinery throughput is under 50% of 1.98 mb/d capacity.

**Upstream and policy.** US Gulf of America deepwater is in a banner year — **Whale (Shell)** online January 2025 hit 100 kb/d in five months; **Anchor (Chevron)** became the first 20K-psi project; **Kaskida (BP)** took FID in July 2024 with first oil in 2029, and **Tiber (BP)** was sanctioned late 2025. **Willow (ConocoPhillips)** has slipped to an early 2029 first oil at a $8.5–9 bn cost; **Pikka (Santos/Repsol)** is on track for 2026 at 80 kb/d peak. Canada oil-sands capex reaches **C$14 bn in 2026 (+5% y/y)**, and on **November 27, 2025** the Carney–Smith "Grand Bargain" **scrapped the planned federal oil-and-gas emissions cap** in exchange for tighter Alberta industrial carbon pricing and Pathways Alliance CCS, also committing to adjust the Oil Tanker Moratorium Act to enable Pacific exports. In Mexico, **Sheinbaum's constitutional amendment** converted Pemex from a "productive state-owned company" to a "state-owned public company," debt was cut 20% in 2025 via $40 bn of government support, and the **Trion project (Woodside 60% op / Pemex 40%)** spudded on March 9, 2026 with a Deepwater Thalassa drillship — first oil 2028, peak ~100 kb/d. Mexico is deliberately cutting crude exports to prioritise domestic refining under its energy-sovereignty doctrine. On the US side, **Trump's January 20, 2025 energy executive orders** and the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 4, 2025)** repealed the IRA's 16⅔% federal onshore royalty, restored the 12.5% MLA minimum, eliminated the methane royalty on post-August 2022 leases, and terminated the §45Y Clean Electricity PTC for facilities placed in service after end-2028.

## Cluster D — China: domestic vs. equity oil abroad

China's domestic crude production hit a **record ~4.3 mb/d (215 Mt)** in 2025, its sixth consecutive annual increase under the 2019–25 Seven-Year Action Plan. But the consumer–producer gap remains vast: imports were **11.65 mb/d** (+5.7% y/y), refinery throughput 14.3 mb/d, and **~900–1,100 kb/d went into strategic/commercial storage** — by end-2025 Kpler put China's total oil stockpile at **1.5 billion barrels**.

**By field**, the **Bohai Bay offshore complex (CNOOC) is now the single largest growth engine** — ~800 kb/d with 12 startups in 2025 alone (Bozhong 26-6 world's largest metamorphic-rock field, Kenli 10-2 heavy oil, Caofeidian 6-4) and two new 100-Mt-class discoveries (Qinhuangdao 29-6, Longkou 25-1). Daqing has declined from 43% of national output in 1995 to 16%, offset by **Changqing (~1.2 mboe/d in the Ordos)**, **Xinjiang (~700–750 kb/d)**, and the **Gulong shale-oil demonstration zone** which topped 1 Mt in 2025 and targets 3 Mt/yr by 2030 off 158 Mt proven reserves. Sinopec's **Jiyang (Bohai Bay onshore) shale demonstration zone completed construction in 2025**.

**Equity oil abroad** totals 1.5–2.0 mb/d, but the structure matters: much of the Iraqi exposure is service-contract volume where net Chinese entitlement after cost recovery is far smaller. The largest positions are **Iraq** (CNPC 37% of Rumaila, PetroChina 45% Halfaya, PetroChina 32.7% West Qurna-1 since Exxon's January 2024 exit, CNPC majority Al-Ahdab — roughly 900–1,000 kb/d gross, 200–300 kb/d net), **UAE ~240 kb/d** (CNPC 8% ADCO onshore, 10% Lower Zakum and Umm Shaif/Nasr), **Brazil ~290–310 kb/d** (CNOOC + CNPC each 9.65% of Libra/Mero and 7.34% of Búzios transfer-of-rights surplus, Sinochem 40% Peregrino), **Guyana ~225 kb/d** (CNOOC's 25% Stabroek stake), **Kazakhstan ~200–220 kb/d** (CNPC 94% AktobeMunaiGas, 67% PetroKazakhstan, 50% MMG, 8.33% Kashagan), **Canada ~95–105 kb/d** (CNOOC/Nexen oil sands), **Venezuela ~40–50 kb/d** (CNPC 40% Sinovensa), and **Africa ~210–220 kb/d** combined (OML 130 Nigeria, Block 17 Angola, Sudan/South Sudan, Ecuador). Chinese NOC activity in Russia is **gas-dominated** — CNPC 20% + Silk Road Fund 9.9% of Yamal LNG, CNPC 10% + CNOOC 10% of Arctic LNG 2 (Train 2 started 2025) — while Sinopec holds 49% of Udmurtneft (~60 kb/d net).

**Import sourcing in 2025** (China GAC / Kpler): Russia ~2.1 mb/d (19%, down 150 kb/d after October OFAC), Saudi Arabia ~1.6 mb/d (falling three straight years), "Malaysia" 1.3 mb/d (mostly rebranded Iranian via STS), Iraq ~1.2 mb/d, Brazil ~0.8 mb/d, with Iran flows tracked at ~1.38 mb/d in Kpler's parallel dataset. Sanctioned crudes accounted for **~22% of 2025 imports**. Teapot refiners in Shandong are the buyer of last resort for Iranian/Russian/Venezuelan barrels, and the new **Yulong Petrochemical (400 kb/d, commissioned September 2024, Shandong)** joined Shenghong and Huayou as mega-complexes pursuing Beijing's "reduce oil products, increase chemicals" policy.

**Narrative highlights.** Iraq and Kazakhstan remain the OPEC+ compliance problem children — the IEA in September 2025 estimated that Iraq, UAE, Kuwait and Kazakhstan were collectively pumping **~1.1 mb/d above quotas**. The reopening of the **Iraq–Türkiye (Kirkuk–Ceyhan) pipeline on September 27, 2025** — after a 2½-year shutdown triggered by ICC arbitration — initially flowed 180–190 kb/d and committed KRG to 400 kb/d under a tripartite deal with eight IOCs (DNO, Gulf Keystone, Genel and others) receiving $14/bbl net via escrow. Kazakhstan meanwhile was briefly rocked by a **Ukrainian drone strike on the CPC's Novorossiysk SPM in December 2025**, cutting output ~6% for two days and forcing diversions to the BTC pipeline (target 2.2 Mtpa) and Druzhba/Ust-Luga KEBCO routes.

Brazil's pre-salt is entering a distinct new phase: Petrobras's **Búzios exceeded 1 mb/d in October 2025**, the **P-78 FPSO started up on December 31, 2025 (180 kb/d)**, **P-79 arrives at Búzios 7 for first oil in August 2026**, and the Equinor-operated **Bacalhau FPSO** came on October 15, 2025 at 220 kb/d — Equinor's largest international asset with CCGT technology delivering ~9 kg CO₂/boe. **IBAMA's October 20, 2025 approval of Petrobras's Foz do Amazonas drilling permit (Block FZA-M-59)** reopened Brazil's equatorial margin to a 5-month drilling campaign and prospectively up to 10 Bn boe of recoverable resources.

Guyana remains the non-OPEC supply story of the decade. **Stabroek hit 900 kb/d on November 12, 2025** — four months after the **ONE GUYANA FPSO at Yellowtail** started up on August 8, four months ahead of schedule. With **Errea Wittu (Uaru) in 2026**, **Jaguar (Whiptail) in 2027** and **Hammerhead (FID September 2025) in 2029**, the consortium (now Exxon 45% / Chevron 30% / CNOOC 25% after Chevron closed its Hess acquisition on July 18, 2025) is on track for 1.3 mb/d by end-2027 and 1.7 mb/d by 2030. Norway's Johan Castberg reached its **220 kb/d plateau within three months** of its March 31, 2025 start in the Barents Sea; Johan Sverdrup continues to deliver ~690 kb/d at plateau; Yggdrasil/NOAKA (Aker BP-operated, NOK 115 bn, first unmanned platforms) is firmly on track for 2027; and Wisting's FID has slipped toward end-2026 as capex rose to NOK 104 bn.

Libya's NOC posted a **10-year production high of 1.37 mb/d in 2025** and **$22 bn in oil revenues (+15–18% y/y)**, reopening the country to foreign capital through the first upstream bid round since 2007 (22 blocks), with Shell, BP, ExxonMobil and Chevron all signing exploration or technical-study agreements, Eni committing **€8 bn** over four years, and OMV making a Sirte Basin discovery in late 2025. Angola's trajectory is being rewritten by **Azule Energy** (the BP–Eni JV), whose **Agogo IWH FPSO came online in July 2025, a full year ahead of schedule**, with 175 kb/d capacity, 36 wells, a $7.8 bn cost base, and the world's first FPSO-integrated CCS unit. Nigeria's structural story is the **Dangote refinery** (650 → 700 kb/d) reaching full capacity in February 2026, creating a domestic demand pull of ~10 cargoes a month for NNPC crude and restoring a measure of industry rationality to onshore operations after Shell's 2025 sale of SPDC to Renaissance Africa and Exxon's sale of MPNU to Seplat. **Bonga North's FID in December 2024** ($5 bn, 110 kb/d, first oil 2030) was the country's first >100 kb/d FID since Egina in 2013. Argentina's **Vaca Muerta** is the closest thing in Latin America to a Permian-style decoupling: **811 kb/d national output in July 2025 (a 26-year high)**, unconventional share at 54% versus 18% in 2019, YPF's corporate breakeven at $45 Brent, and the **VMOS pipeline** under construction to Punta Colorada — together with a reactivated Trasandino line to Chile — targeting 1 mb/d of exports.

## How geopolitical tensions are reshaping supply chains in 2025/2026

**Three structural shifts define the year.** First, the sanctions regime on Russian oil has moved from financial instrument to physical enforcement: Trump's October 22, 2025 SDN of Rosneft and Lukoil, the EU's $47.60 price cap, UAV strikes on shadow-fleet tankers in the Black Sea and Mediterranean, and the December naval quarantine on Venezuela have blown Merey and Urals discounts out to $21 and $25.80 per barrel respectively. Net effect: teapot refiners in Shandong running at 45–46% utilisation in early 2025, Indian refiners rejecting sanctioned cargoes, and Iran's product exports up in 2025 despite production drama — **Iran posted a 7.5-year output high in September 2025** and UANI's vessel-tracked export revenue of $45.7 bn is a record. Russia's federal deficit reached **2.6% of GDP in 2025** — its largest since 2020 — and oil-and-gas revenues fell 24% y/y with VAT hiked to 22% for 2026.

**Second, the Middle East is simultaneously unwinding OPEC+ cuts and rebuilding Hormuz bypass capacity.** The April–September 2025 restoration of 2.2 mb/d returned roughly 9.5 mb/d to Saudi Arabia's sustainable capacity and pushed UAE output above 3.1 mb/d en route to an announced 5.0 mb/d by 2027. Saudi Petroline's claimed capacity of 7 mb/d and ADNOC's plan to double ADCOP through a second 1.5 mb/d line by 2026–27 are responses to a live threat: the April 2026 EIA STEO explicitly models a Hormuz disruption that pushes Brent to $115/bbl in Q2 2026. **Iraq's Ceyhan pipeline reopened in September 2025**; Kazakhstan's CPC was hit by a Ukrainian drone in December and is diversifying to BTC.

**Third, the non-OPEC supply stack is rotating decisively toward the Atlantic basin**. Guyana's Stabroek hit 900 kb/d in November 2025 and is on a glidepath to 1.7 mb/d by 2030. Brazilian pre-salt added three major FPSOs (P-78, Bacalhau, Alexandre de Gusmão) and launched the equatorial-margin era with IBAMA's Foz do Amazonas licence. Suriname's GranMorgu, Senegal's Sangomar and Côte d'Ivoire's Baleine are proof-of-concept for a new Atlantic Margin fairway — together with Namibia's emerging Orange Basin — that will add **well over 1 mb/d of high-quality, low-cost barrels** between 2024 and 2028. Argentina's Vaca Muerta is on track to more than double to 1.5 mb/d by 2030 behind the VMOS pipeline.

## Conclusion: a fragmented barrel market living on borrowed time

The 2025/2026 oil market looks balanced on the surface — OPEC+ can still flood it with spare capacity, US output remains near record, and global stocks rose every quarter of 2025 — but the plumbing beneath is more fragile than any period since the early 2000s. **Roughly one barrel in seven now trades outside normal commercial and insurance infrastructure**, via a shadow fleet that has grown faster than either sanctions enforcement or the available sanctioned-buyer base. The October 22 OFAC action against Rosneft and Lukoil was the first moment in which Washington treated the private Russian majors as financial pariahs rather than price-capped counterparties; the market response — a $20 widening of the Urals discount in under eight weeks — gave the clearest price signal yet that *secondary* enforcement, not just primary designations, has begun to bite.

Three non-obvious insights fall out of the synthesis. **The Permian plateau is real**: US output will decline in 2026 for the first time in five years despite $250+ bn of consolidation, because post-merger majors are optimising for shareholder returns at $88–95 breakevens, not for volume at $62. **Mexico is the closest thing to a geopolitical wildcard outside the sanctioned triangle** — a 1.6 mb/d producer actively cutting crude exports by policy choice, with Pemex's future contingent on a $24.7 bn investment plan populated by small local contractors rather than IOCs. **The single most under-priced supply-side event of 2026** may be not NFE LNG or Jafurah Phase 1 but the completion of Argentina's **Vaca Muerta Sur pipeline** and the subsequent arrival of up to 1 mb/d of exportable Atlantic-basin light sweet crude — a volume comparable to Norway's entire seaborne export book, sourced from a country with no OPEC quota and a reform-minded government offering RIGI tax incentives. The sanctions architecture, the GCC capacity build, and the Atlantic-margin surge are each, individually, the biggest story of the year. Together, they describe an oil market whose old pole stars — OPEC+ discipline, Russian–European integration, a US-led G7 price consensus — have all dimmed at once, with nothing yet fully in place to replace them.

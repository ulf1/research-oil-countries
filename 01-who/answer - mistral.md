## **Geopolitical Analysis: Shifting Supply Chains in 2025/2026**

### **1. Sanctioned Countries: The Shadow Fleet Era**
- **Russia, Iran, and Venezuela** have **institutionalized** the use of **shadow fleets** (now ~3,300 vessels, 6–7% of global flows) to bypass Western sanctions. Key hubs (Fujairah, Black Sea, Eastern Mediterranean) act as **redistribution points** for sanctioned cargoes.
- **China and India** remain the **primary buyers** of discounted sanctioned oil, with China absorbing **~50% of Iranian exports** and **~25% of Russian seaborne exports**.
- **U.S. enforcement** (e.g., tanker seizures, OFAC sanctions) has **increased risks** but not stopped flows. The **cost of evasion** (e.g., ship-to-ship transfers, spoofed AIS, non-standard insurance) is now **baked into the price** of sanctioned oil.

### **2. GCC: Spare Capacity and Diversification**
- **Saudi Arabia and UAE** hold **~3–4 mb/d of spare capacity**, but **Strait of Hormuz disruptions** (March–April 2026) exposed vulnerabilities, with **~20 mb/d of Gulf oil temporarily inaccessible**.
- **Diversification projects** (e.g., Saudi Vision 2030, UAE’s We the UAE 2031, Qatar’s North Field LNG) are **accelerating**, but oil remains central to fiscal revenues. **Non-oil GDP growth** is outpacing oil in some GCC states (e.g., UAE, Saudi Arabia).
- **OPEC+ dynamics**: GCC producers are **gradually unwinding voluntary cuts**, but **geopolitical risks** (e.g., Houthi attacks, Iran-Israel tensions) could trigger supply shocks.

### **3. North America: Shale Resilience and Pipeline Bottlenecks**
- **U.S. shale** remains the **global swing producer**, with **Permian Basin** driving record output. **Efficiency gains** (e.g., longer laterals, advanced completions) have offset **rig declines**.
- **Pipeline infrastructure** is expanding, but **bottlenecks** (e.g., Permian egress, Gulf Coast congestion) persist. **LNG exports** are a growing outlet for associated gas.
- **Canada and Mexico** are **stable suppliers** to the U.S., with Canada’s oil sands and Mexico’s heavy crude filling niche roles.

### **4. China: Domestic Stability vs. Overseas Expansion**
- **Domestic production** is **stable at ~4.3 mb/d**, but **import dependence** remains high (~73%). China’s NOCs are **aggressively expanding overseas** (e.g., Brazil, Guyana, Kazakhstan) to secure equity oil.
- **Energy security** is the priority, with **strategic storage, LNG imports, and pipeline investments** (e.g., Power of Siberia, Myanmar-China) reducing reliance on volatile Middle Eastern supply.

### **5. Other Producers: New Frontiers and Decline**
- **Brazil and Guyana** are the **fastest-growing producers**, with **deepwater pre-salt and Stabroek Block** projects adding **~1 mb/d of new supply by 2027**.
- **Africa (Nigeria, Angola, Libya)** is **recovering** after years of decline, but **infrastructure and political risks** persist.
- **Minor producers** (e.g., Congo, Ecuador, Vietnam) are **stable or declining**, with limited upside potential.

## **Key Takeaways for Supply Chain Shifts**
1. **Sanctioned Oil Flows Are Here to Stay**: The **shadow fleet** has become a **permanent feature** of global oil trade, with China and India as the primary offtakers. **Enforcement is reactive, not preventive**.
2. **GCC Spare Capacity is Critical but Vulnerable**: The **Strait of Hormuz remains a chokepoint**, and **GCC diversification** is accelerating but not yet enough to offset oil dependence.
3. **North America is the New Swing Producer**: **U.S. shale** and **Canadian oil sands** are filling gaps left by OPEC+ cuts and Middle Eastern disruptions.
4. **China’s NOCs Are Globalizing**: **PetroChina, Sinopec, CNOOC** are **securing equity oil abroad** to reduce import risks, while domestic production holds steady.
5. **New Frontiers (Brazil, Guyana, Kazakhstan) Are Reshaping Supply**: These countries are **adding ~1.5 mb/d of new production by 2027**, partially offsetting declines in mature regions.

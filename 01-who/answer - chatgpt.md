## What is changing now

The biggest supply-chain shift is the rerouting of barrels away from the Strait of Hormuz risk zone. EIA says flows through Hormuz in 2024 and 1Q25 carried more than one-quarter of global seaborne oil trade and about one-fifth of global oil and product consumption, while Reuters reports that Middle East exports dropped at least 60% when the chokepoint stayed mostly closed. That has raised the value of alternative routes such as Saudi Arabia’s East-West pipeline, pushed more barrels into floating storage, and elevated tanker rates. ([U.S. Energy Information Administration][7])

For sanctioned producers, the market is becoming more segmented rather than fully blocked. Russia continues to move crude eastward at a discount through an aging shadow fleet, Iran is relying on storage and clandestine liftings toward China, and Venezuela’s exports remain tied to sanctions permissions, diluent supply, and debt-swap structures. That means headline supply may hold up better than logistics suggest, but the “last-mile” cost of moving barrels is rising. ([Reuters][8])

Macro-wise, the IMF now sees a weaker 2026 backdrop for oil exporters and importers alike: MENA growth is only 1.4% in 2026, Sub-Saharan Africa 4.3%, the U.S. 2.3%, and Canada 0.9%, while the IEA cut 2026 global oil demand and refining-run expectations in April 2026 because of the shock. The result is a market that is still ample on paper, but far more fragile in transport and geopolitics. ([IMF][9])
